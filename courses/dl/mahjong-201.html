ðŸ€„THE SCAN-SHAPE-COMMIT-DEFEND FRAMEWORK

This is your decision-making loop for EVERY game.

ðŸ€„SCAN

Read the table + card options

Before choosing a hand:

  • What tiles did I keep through Charleston?
  • What tiles am I drawing?
  • What sections of the card match my tiles?
  • What are opponents likely playing (based on Charleston)?

ðŸ€„SHAPE

Build the most flexible version of your hand

While keeping options alive:

  • Hold "bridge tiles" that fit multiple hands
  • Keep 2-3 possible hands in play
  • Avoid locking in too early
  • Build melds that overlap between hands

ðŸ€„COMMIT

Choose the line and speed up

When you have clear progress:

  • Select your final hand
  • Start building aggressively
  • Use jokers strategically
  • Expose when it accelerates without killing you

ðŸ€„DEFEND

Stop feeding wins and protect position

When opponents are close:

  • Read danger tiles
  • Discard safely
  • Slow down if needed
  • Finish second instead of donating a win
This framework applies to EVERY SINGLE GAME.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 1: HAND SELECTION LIKE A PRO

ðŸ€„The Five Factors

1. Tile Density (Most Important)

Which tiles exist in abundance?

Example:

  • You have: 4-4-4-5-6-7-8 Bams
  • That's 7 Bams in a concentrated range
  • HIGH DENSITY = Look for Bam runs or pungs
Low density:
  • You have: 2 Dot, 7 Crak, North, Flower
  • Random tiles = HARD to build
Rule: Choose hands supported by your tile density.
2. Joker Count
  • 0 jokers = Choose hands with common tiles
  • 1-2 jokers = Standard flexibility
  • 3+ jokers = Aggressive/faster hands
Why: Jokers let you complete difficult melds. More jokers = more ambitious hands become viable.
3. Early Draws

Your first 3-4 draws tell you a lot.

Drawing:

  • Pairs? â†’ Pair-heavy hands
  • Runs? â†’ Run-based hands
  • Honors? â†’ Winds/Dragons hands
Don't ignore early signals.
4. Charleston Outcomes

What came back to you?

  • Got back Dots? â†’ Dots are available
  • Got back Winds? â†’ Others passing honors
  • Got random junk? â†’ Stay flexible longer
5. Avoiding "Trap Hands"

Trap hands LOOK good but are statistical nightmares.

Red flags:
  • Requires 5+ exact singles
  • Needs tiles that rarely get discarded
  • Depends on perfect draws
  • Has no joker flexibility
Example trap hand:
  • FFFF 1111 9999 DDDD (Flowers, Ones, Nines, Dragons)
  • Looks cool, but you need 4 flowers + exact pungs + dragons
  • Probability = VERY LOW
Better: Choose hands with flexibility built in.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 2: SHAPE - TILE EFFICIENCY + FLEXIBLE BUILDS

ðŸ€„Keeping Options Alive

The 2-Hand Minimum Rule

Until you're 60% toward one hand, keep TWO hands viable.

Example:

  • Hand A: 111 222 333 DDD (Ones, Twos, Threes, Dragons)
  • Hand B: 1111 2222 3333 (Ones, Twos, Threesâ€”kongs)
Your tiles: 1-1-1-2-2-3-3-3-Red Dragon-Red Dragon
You can build toward EITHER hand until you commit.

ðŸ€„How to Hold "Bridge Tiles"

Bridge tiles fit multiple hands.

Example:

  • You have: 5-6-7 Dots
  • Hand A needs: 5-6-7-8 run
  • Hand B needs: 4-5-6-7 run
The 6-7 are bridge tiles. They overlap both hands.
Keep these as long as possible.

ðŸ€„When a Hand Is Secretly Dead

Dead hand signs:
  • You need 4 specific tiles, and 3 have been discarded
  • Two opponents exposed your tiles
  • You're on turn 15 and have made zero progress
  • You'd need perfect draws for 6+ consecutive turns
Don't be stubborn. Dead is dead.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 3: EXPOSURE STRATEGY (THE REAL RULES)

ðŸ€„When Exposures Help

Expose when:
  1. You're 3-4 tiles from winning
  • Close enough that locking in is safe
  1. The tile is rare and won't come again
  • Example: You need the 4th Red Dragon, someone throws it
  1. You have jokers backing you up
  • Jokers give you flexibility even after exposing
  1. You can finish in 2-4 more draws
  • Speed mattersâ€”get there first

ðŸ€„When Exposures Lock You In (Dangerous)

Don't expose if:
  1. You're still deciding between 2-3 hands
  • Exposing kills your options
  1. You need multiple exact singles later
  • Exposing won't help; you'll still need perfect draws
  1. You're early (before turn 8-10)
  • Too soon; you don't have enough information
  1. The table is moving slow
  • No pressure = stay concealed longer

ðŸ€„The "One Exposure" Advantage

One exposure puts pressure on opponents without fully revealing your hand.

Example:

  • You expose: 6-6-6 Craks
  • Opponents know you're on Craks, but don't know:
  • Are you doing runs?
  • Kongs?
  • Mixed suits?
Strategic ambiguity = power.

ðŸ€„When to Stay Concealed Longer

Stay concealed when:
  • You're winning the race (ahead of others)
  • You want to hide your direction
  • You can finish without exposing
  • Opponents are aggressive (let them expose and fight)
Concealed wins are often safer wins.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 4: JOKER MANAGEMENT (INTERMEDIATE LEVEL)

ðŸ€„Playing Joker-Rich vs. Joker-Poor

With 0-1 Jokers:
  • Choose common-tile hands
  • Avoid trap hands
  • Focus on hands with high tile availability
  • Play conservatively
With 2-3 Jokers:
  • Standard strategy
  • Use jokers for hard-to-complete melds
  • Moderate aggression
With 4+ Jokers:
  • AGGRESSIVE strategy
  • Target faster hands
  • Expose earlier (you have backup)
  • Push tempo

ðŸ€„When to Chase a Joker Swap

In Dallas Mahjong (blanks), you can swap jokers from exposed melds.
Chase a swap when:
  • You have the exact tile
  • The joker solves a critical problem
  • It's your turn and low-risk
Don't chase when:
  • The swap doesn't meaningfully help
  • You'd have to break your hand to get the tile
  • The player is close to Mahjong (they might win before you use it)

ðŸ€„How to Avoid Joker Bait Mistakes

Joker bait = exposing jokers too early, making them swap targets
Protect jokers by:
  • Exposing joker melds LAST (when you're 1-2 tiles from Mahjong)
  • Using real tiles for early exposures
  • Keeping joker melds concealed longer
Example:

You have:

  • 5-5-5 Bams (real tiles)
  • 8-8-Joker Craks (one joker)
Expose the Bams first. Save the joker meld for when you're close.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 5: CHARLESTON STRATEGY (INTERMEDIATE)

ðŸ€„Passing with Intention

Intermediate Charleston isn't random cleanupâ€”it's strategic shaping.

ðŸ€„Pass 1 (Right): Information Gathering

What to pass:
  • Tiles from suits you're definitely not using
  • Isolated honors
What to watch:
  • What came back? (Signals what's available)

ðŸ€„Pass 2 (Across): Directional Commitment

What to pass:
  • Tiles from your weaker hand option
  • Tiles you're 80% sure you won't need
What to watch:
  • Are you getting your suit back? (Bad sign)
  • Are you getting honors? (Others not using them)

ðŸ€„Pass 3 (Left): Final Refinement

What to pass:
  • Last cleanup of unusable tiles
What to keep:
  • Jokers (always)
  • Tiles for your top 2 hands
  • Bridge tiles

ðŸ€„"Information Passing" vs. "Tile Dumping"

Tile dumping (beginner):
  • "I don't want this, pass it"
Information passing (intermediate):
  • "If I pass this and get it back, I know it's abundant"
  • "If I pass honors and get honors back, few are using them"
You're gathering data, not just cleaning.

ðŸ€„What Your Passes Reveal (To Others)

Passing lots of one suit = you're not using that suit

Example:

  • You pass 3 Bams every round
  • Smart opponents know: Don't discard Bams later (you won't call them)
Strategic consideration: Mix your passes slightly to avoid being too readable.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 6: TABLE READING 101

ðŸ€„What Discards Tell You

Early discards (Turns 1-8):
  • Random cleanup
  • Suits they're not using
  • Isolated honors
Mid-game discards (Turns 9-15):
  • More intentional
  • Tiles that don't fit their hand
  • Defensive throws (if someone exposed)
Late-game discards (Turns 16+):
  • Highly defensive
  • "Safe" tiles (already thrown)
  • Desperation (breaking their hand to avoid feeding)

ðŸ€„Identifying Likely Sections of the Card

Look at exposures:
Exposed: 3-3-3 Dots and 7-7-7 Dots
  • Likely: Like Numbers section (same number, different suits)
  • Possible: Consecutive section with Dot runs
Exposed: NNNN (4 Norths)
  • Likely: Winds & Dragons section
Exposed: 2-4-6-8 Craks
  • Likely: 2468 section
Use exposures to narrow down their hand range.

ðŸ€„Spotting Who Is Close

Danger signs:
  • 2+ exposures showing
  • Calling tiles quickly
  • Discarding "safe" tiles only
  • Looking confident (body language)
  • Has jokers exposed
When you spot danger, adjust your discards.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 7: TIMING - WHEN TO PIVOT VS. WHEN TO COMMIT

ðŸ€„The 3 Pivot Triggers

1. No Progress After 12 Draws
  • You're not getting closer
  • Your tiles aren't coming
  • Time to switch
2. Your Tiles Are Being Discarded
  • Multiple players throwing your suit
  • Sign: it's dead
  • Find another path
3. Two Players Exposed in Your Territory
  • They've claimed your tiles
  • You're now fighting for scraps
  • Pivot to different tiles

ðŸ€„The "Last Responsible Moment" Rule

Commit as late as possible, but not too late.
Last responsible moment = when:
  • You have 2 complete melds
  • You can see a clear path to finish
  • Waiting longer doesn't help
Too early: Turn 5 (not enough info)
Too late: Turn 18 with nothing (should have pivoted by turn 12)
Just right: Turn 10-14 (with clear progress)

ðŸ€„Avoiding the Sunk Cost Trap

Sunk cost fallacy:

"I've been building this hand for 15 turns, I can't give up now!"

Reality:

Those 15 turns are GONE. They don't matter.

What matters: Can you win from HERE?

If no â†’ Pivot

If yes â†’ Commit

Don't throw good turns after bad.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 8: DEFEND - STOP FEEDING THE TABLE

ðŸ€„Safe-ish Discards

No discard is 100% safe, but some are safer:
Safest:
  • Tiles already discarded 3+ times
  • Tiles nobody has called
  • Tiles outside exposed ranges
Dangerous:
  • Tiles matching exposures
  • Tiles in "hot" suits
  • Tiles nobody has thrown (everyone might want them)

ðŸ€„Danger Tiles

High-risk throws:
When someone exposed Bam runs:
  • Other Bams (they might need more)
When someone exposed 5-5-5:
  • Other 5s in different suits (if playing Like Numbers)
When someone exposed Dragons:
  • Other Dragons (might need more)

ðŸ€„How to Play When Someone Is Exposed and Hot

Defensive checklist:
  1. Stop discarding tiles in their range
  • If they exposed Craks, avoid Craks
  1. Discard tiles already heavily thrown
  • Safe-ish choices
  1. Slow down your own hand if needed
  • Better to finish second than donate a win
  1. Watch for their final tile needs
  • If they need one more tile, don't be the one to throw it

ðŸ€„The Defensive Discard Sequence

Safest to riskiest:
  1. Tiles discarded 4+ times (nearly dead)
  2. Tiles discarded 2-3 times (probably not wanted)
  3. Honors nobody called (if not in Winds/Dragons hands)
  4. Edge tiles (1s, 9s) if no runs showing
  5. Middle tiles (4, 5, 6) ONLY if confident
When in doubt, throw what's been thrown.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 9: ENDGAME DECISIONS

ðŸ€„How to Finish a Hand

You're 2-3 tiles away. Now what?
Aggressive finish:
  • Expose remaining melds
  • Use jokers
  • Call any helpful tile
  • Push tempo
When to use: You're ahead, or table is slow
Conservative finish:
  • Stay concealed
  • Wait for perfect draws
  • Don't expose unless critical
When to use: Opponents are also close, or you're slightly behind

ðŸ€„When to Push

Push when:
  • You're closest to winning
  • You have jokers
  • Opponents are slower
  • You can finish in 2-4 draws

ðŸ€„When to Freeze and Survive

Freeze when:
  • Two players are exposed and racing
  • You can't win
  • Your goal is "don't donate a win"
Freeze tactics:
  • Discard only safe tiles
  • Break your own hand if needed
  • Play for second place (or wall game)
Sometimes the best move is NOT winningâ€”it's NOT LOSING.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 10: DALLAS MAHJONG / BLANKS STRATEGY ADD-ON

ðŸ€„How Blanks Change Risk

With joker swapping:
  • Players can acquire jokers mid-game
  • Hands speed up unpredictably
  • "Safe" tiles become less safe
Implication: Games are faster and more volatile.

ðŸ€„How to Defend When Blanks Exist

Standard defense assumes: Players keep the jokers they started with
With blanks: Players can GET jokers from exposures
Defense adjustment:
  • Assume anyone can suddenly speed up
  • Don't rely on "they don't have jokers" logic
  • Play slightly more aggressively (since others will too)

ðŸ€„When Blanks Create Fake Safety

Fake safety:

"They need 3 more tiles and have no jokersâ€”I'm safe"

Reality with blanks:

"They could swap a joker this turn and suddenly need only 1 tile"

Adjustment: Stay cautious even when opponents seem far behind.

ðŸ€„CHAPTER 11: INTERMEDIATE RULES OF THUMB (30+)

ðŸ€„Hand Selection

  1. Tile density beats hand beauty
  2. Jokers multiply your optionsâ€”use them wisely
  3. Avoid hands requiring 4+ exact singles
  4. Trust your early draws
  5. If you can't name 2 viable hands post-Charleston, you're in trouble

ðŸ€„Charleston

  1. Never pass jokers
  2. Pass isolated tiles first, suits second, honors last
  3. Watch what comes backâ€”it signals availability
  4. Keep bridge tiles that fit multiple hands
  5. Don't commit mentally until after Charleston

ðŸ€„Exposures

  1. One exposure = pressure; three exposures = transparency
  2. Expose rare tiles, not common ones
  3. Don't expose before turn 10 unless you're loaded
  4. If exposing locks you into needing exact singles, don't expose
  5. Concealed wins are often better wins

ðŸ€„Jokers

  1. Save jokers for pungs/kongs, not runs (when possible)
  2. Don't expose joker melds until you're 1-2 tiles from Mahjong
  3. With 4+ jokers, play aggressively
  4. With 0 jokers, play conservatively
  5. Joker swaps (blanks) are only worth it if they solve a real problem

ðŸ€„Table Reading

  1. Early discards = cleanup; late discards = defense
  2. Watch exposure patterns to guess card sections
  3. If two players throw the same tile back-to-back, it's probably dead
  4. Silence (no exposures) often means strong concealed hands
  5. Exposed players are easier to read

ðŸ€„Timing

  1. Pivot by turn 12 if you have no progress
  2. Commit when you have 2 complete melds
  3. The hand that's 70% built beats the hand that's 30% perfect
  4. Don't chase perfection; chase completion
  5. Wall games (no winner) often result from everyone waiting too long

Question: Do you call and expose the pung of 7 Dots? Why or why not?
Answer: NO. You're not close to winning (still need multiple tiles), and exposing now reveals you're on Dots/Craks without meaningful progress. Stay concealed and keep building.

ðŸ€„Scenario 3: Pivot or Commit?

  • 2-2 Craks
  • North, South, West
  • Joker
Discards show: Bams have been heavily thrown (8+ Bam tiles discarded)

Answer: LAST. Expose the real-tile pungs first (3-3-3 and 2-2-2). Only expose the joker meld when you're 1 tile from Mahjong. This prevents opponents from swapping your joker (in Dallas rules) and keeps your flexibility.
